From Post to Posts: The Story of Information Spread

Manish Shukla

Abstract

The problem is to identify and model the impact of technology on dissemination of news in a society, across various time periods in history. We ﬁrst formally model the social networks as directed graphs where each node is a person and there is an edge between two people if at least one of them is linked to the other. Each person holds some value for an information and Gossip acts as the communication protocol that dictates how information ﬂows through the edges. To simulate the real-world scenario, we test against a variety of random graph models and show that the Holme- Kim Random Graph most accurately captures our assumptions. Using past data, we estimate the diﬀerent parameter values for various technologies such as newspapers, radio and television and internet. We then release ’information’ from a few nodes and compare the rate of spread for the various technologies. We measure this by mapping the fraction of population covered. We observe that the rate of information spread has gotten much faster and the coverage has increased as well. We also observe that if any piece of information is not deemed important by a large fraction of the population, its spread is deterred.

1Introduction

The complex interactions between communication technologies and social structures have resulted in a culturally and institutionally diverse society. The modern age is driven by information and rapidly changing technology has made it increasingly diﬃcult to identify its impacts and consequences on society[7]. Network science has therefore become an important ﬁeld of study today.

The collective research eﬀort so far has focused on the social processes that led to the emergence of technology as a material culture, the study of digital information and its social uses and applications[3]. However, observation of the practices of information networks as an organizational form can hugely contribute to our understanding of the world in a unique way. In our paper, we aim to do this through an analysis of information dissemination under changing landscapes of technology and social connectivity.

We argue that the ethnocentrism of a uniﬁed, global societal network is largely erro- neous. To be precise, there are some strikingly common features across these paradigms as we present here. But there is tremendous diversity in what is valued as useful and important. We show that depending on the historical time-period, context, choice of technology, culture, economy and media, the diﬀusion of information occurs in diﬀerent ways.

2Assumptions

Our goal is to design a model that simulates information spread in social networks across 5 periods from 1870 to till present times. It is not immediately clear what is to be solved here since this is a very broad problem statement. We ﬁrst present some clarifying deﬁnitions and assumptions in order to restate the problem and narrow our focus.

•Anybody can be an Information Source. Every person in the social network is capable of producing, creating and distributing information. The authenticity of the information is that person’s responsibility.

•Preferential Attachment. If a person is very highly connected, then there is a higher probability of new people also getting connected to him. This is a well- documented phenomenon and we make this assumption because it leads to power-law distributions in real networks([1], [6], [4], [2]). For example, the circulation statistics of the leading newspapers around the world follows a power-law distribution as can be seen in Fig 1.

Limiting Assumptions

We also make the following limiting assumptions for the purposes of our current model.

•Information Transmission is Noise-Free. When information spreads from per- son to person, it does not get modiﬁed and the transmission is not corrupted. For instance, if Alice passes the news that Coldplay is performing in India, this is exactly what gets passed around.

•The representational system of information is independent of persons and models. We assume that the information is in some standard form that is accessible to everyone. We do this in order to observe the eﬀects of the technology on information spread without involving formal semantic structures.

3Constructing a Model of Information Society

What is News?

While modelling technology and its impact in information dissemination, we avoid mak- ing the subjective judgement on what is ought to be called news and what is not. Amidst a variety of claimed categorizations of news, we take a more extrinsic viewpoint in clas- sifying any piece of diﬀusing information as news.

We take a piece of diﬀusing information as news, for all practical purposes, if

•it has a value for members of society, and

•members have tendency to share it.

In the absence of latter, that is information for which individuals do not have an inclination to share, it would not be news but could be expertise knowledge or trade secret. While diﬀering understandings of value in the former condition would draw dif- fering boundaries between news and entertainment. However, solely from a practical and extrinsic viewpoint, there is no distinction between the news of a political assassination and that of release of a new Justin Bieber album, if all forms of public interest are seen as expressions of some intrinsic value.

3.1How News Spreads

News is any information that spreads. Some news may be more relevant to a lot of people, and others news to a select few. When people hear some piece of news, they either choose to believe the news or discard it. What some person ﬁnds important, need not be as important for every other person. People pass on information that they value. The rate at which the news spreads depends on the following factors:

•Value of Information - This value is decided by the social, cultural and economic attributes placed on it by the person and/or society. The more a person values it, the more eﬀort she will expend to pass it to others.

•Centrality - The importance and connectedness of the person passing on the in- formation.

•Clustering - The size of the clusters in the network and the way they are formed impacts how quickly news spreads within a cluster and also between clusters.

•Distance - People far away from each other will generally take longer to communi- cate with each other. This however, can be oﬀset by their position in the network and other factors listed above.

Apart from these, technology is the primary factor aﬀecting the spread of information. We discuss this in Section 3.2.

3.2Economy and Technology

Conversation is an immensely crucial aspect of the human condition, and the desire to express and communicate are fundamental to what makes us human. Conversation and subsequent exchange of information forms the basis of existence of society, functioning of markets and emergence of political institutions, among other things.

However, Humans, in physical reality, are prisoners of their geography. There are strong limitations as a consequence of our geographic locality and costs for transporta- tion. However technological progress impacts communication, or eﬀective channels for information exchange, by transforming the economics of medium of conversation. Com- munication Technology changes the shape and structure of how conversation takes place in society, eﬀectively allowing for liberation of the information layer of society from phys- ical constraints, by providing competitive alternatives.

In order to assess the role that Communication Technologies play in information dis- semination, and arrive at a theory of technology in communication, we make some general observations on the path of progress that has been made in communication in the last few centuries. While generally speaking technology plays an enabling role by lowering the cost of transferring information, the more speciﬁc ways in which it does so include:

1.Social Connectivity - An important role of technology has been to enable human civilization in overcoming the limitations of locality. Allows for people to interact and connect with new people even more than before. This allows for formation of communities beyond those governed by geography. Provides a boost to emergence of a virtual space of communication layer, with increasingly less dependencies on locality. Small Worlds. As a consequence of increasing triangulation, as recorded by Watts and Strogatz.

2.Long Distance Conversations - While, relationships and acquaintances are of- ten concentrated in small communities, or clusters in social networks, governed by geographical proximity, as a result of social interactions, many weak links are also formed across such clusters. And their weakness arises from the apparent cost of transmitting information across large distances, against the ease of meetings. This advantage is strictly in terms of both the economic cost as well as time or latency. The role of technology in bringing down the cost of communication across distances, has a direct consequence in the volume of information exchanged between individ- uals who do not share proximity. The change has been gradual from the time of horseback riders to postmen, and further to Whatsapp. We model this by providing for a coeﬃcient of technology γ, that reduces the impact of communities on the weakness of links.

3.Media Outreach - The advent of technology, and reducing rates of publishing also allow for greater outreach for those who wish to express. The lowering of costs in fact has a cascading eﬀect on the inatitutions of publishing themselves. For instance, while earlier the operations of scale for publishing were such that it required a ﬁrm and contractual union to operate on larger capital, today a Twitter celebirty can simply have more followers than subscribers of the former. This causes decentralization of publishing as well.

However, with the dropping of cost of mass communication, how much one could achieve with similar money has also increased. Today the breadth of subscription that a single ﬁrm or private individual has increased signiﬁcantly, which can be accounted in the factors involved in generation of random graph.

4.1Simulating Real-World Networks

In order to simulate networks, we tested several types of random graph generators against our initial assumptions as follows:

1.Erdos-Renyi Graph: The G(n, p) model chooses each edge between every pair of two nodes with probability p. However, due to the inherent randomness, there were no good clusters formed and preferential attachment was not achieved either.

2.Watts-Strogatz Graph: This G(n, k, p) model, is built by joining each node with k of its nearest neighbours and rewiring edges with probability p. This allowed for formation of clusters, but preferential attachment was not observed. The Newman- Watts-Strogatz Graph is an improvement on the standard model in terms of clus- tering coeﬃcient.

3.Barabasi Albert Random Graph: This G(n, m) model adds a new node to m already existing nodes. This displayed good preferential attachment, however, due clusters were not observed. This is insuﬃcient for the pre-mass communication era where the spread of information was far more localized.

4.Holme-Kim Random Graph: This G(n, m, p) model adds m random edges for each node with a probability p of adding a triangle after adding an edge. We observed that this resulted in graphs with good clusters and preferential attachment properties.

5Strengths and Weaknesses

5.1Strengths

•Simplicity. The model is a simpliﬁed model that allows us to focus on the role that technological progress plays in communication. By focussing on ‘what’s instead of ‘how’s we are able to talk about the role of progress without being concerned about the exact manner of use.

•Another strength of our approach is the use of Holme and Nim method, in a manner that is extendable to many more parameters in evolution.

5.2Weaknesses

•Intractability : As a result of various relevant graph problems required for compu- tation of average, many relevant quantities become intractable, ie computable in

•Linguistic Faultlines : Our model does not account for the variety of disparities and cracks in the society as a result of the linguistic gaps. Our graphs are usually well connected as opposed to real world networks where linguistics causes less connec- tivity between many communities. It would be interesting to observe, however, how the role of technology in overcoming those challenges are observed.

•Trust and Bias : Our model is a very simple approach that abstracted out the content and form of the information that is travelling. While the it would still follow very patterns in real life, individuals still make a lot of judgement about a piece of information from its apparent source, the form of the post, and tne pre- existing notions of such individuals. Information is not static as it moves across the network, but gets skewed by biases and opinions as well as expanded original comments and new knowledge you are willing to put.

•Conviction Parameter : As discussed, it is important in most scenarios that the rate of information transfer not only depend on some intrinsic valuation of information and the ease of communication across the channel, but also on the conviction of the source node.

•Economic Value in Mass Media : While the model is homogenous in its treatment of high-density nodes (media) and others (consumers of information). We argue how emergence of media houses is,at a fundamental level is similar to emergence of Quora or Stack Overﬂow celebrities. However, ﬁrms that exchange money for promise of information also behave substantially diﬀerent in some ways which need to be captured.
